Matrices

  • Orthogonal matrix
    • Ref. It is a square matrix in which all of the vectors that make up the matrix are orthonormal to each other. This must hold in terms of all rows and all columns, i.e. the transpose of an orthogonal matrix is itself an orthogonal matrix. In terms of geometry, orthogonal means that two vectors are perpendicular to each other. When the magnitude (length) of each vector is equal to 1, then they are said to be orthonormal.

  • Geometric representation of matrices
    • Let's take a ($3\times1$) matrix. Each row represents the x,y,z components of a single 3d vector respectively.

  • Determinant of a matrix
    • Ref. It gives an idea of how a matrix (i.e. a set of related vectors; 2 if 2d, 3 if 3d) is transformed in terms of:
    • Area (or volume if 3d)
    • Amount the area/volume is squished by
      • The individual vectors slant. We can have the case where the area/volume remains the same, but it isn't uniform throughout
    • Orientation of the vectors
      • A negative determinant means the area/volume has been flipped by $180^0$

    Determinant calculation

    In 2D (2d matrices), the determinant is calculated as follows: $$\begin{bmatrix}a & b\\c & d\end{bmatrix} \rightarrow (a*d) - (b*c)$$ $a*d$ - represents how much the area is scaled by
    $b*c$ - represents how much the area is squished by
