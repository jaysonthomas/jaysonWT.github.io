Overview

Forward dynamics involves finding the joint accelerations $\ddot{\theta}$, given the current joint velocities $\dot{\theta}$, positions $\theta$ and the torques $\tau$ applied at each joint. It is useful for simulation.

Inverse dynamics invloves finding the joint forces and torques needed to create an acceleration for given joint positions and velocities. It is useful in control of robots.

In the forward part, we think about the end application. In the inverse part, we think about what needs to be done to achieve in the forward part. We look through 2 approaches to solving the forward/inverse dynamics problems.

Lagrangian formulation

This method is conceptually simple and is based on the kinetic and potential energy of the robot.

Lagrangian formulation

$$ L(\theta, \dot{\theta}) = \underbrace{K(\theta, \dot{\theta})}_ {\substack{\text{Kinetic} \\\\\\ \text{energy}}} - \underbrace{P(\theta)}_ {\substack{\text{Potential} \\\\\\ \text{energy}}} $$ The potential energy only depends on the configuration $\theta$.

We won't derive the Lagrangian equations of motion, which can be found in many textbooks on mechanics. We'll just state the result: the vector of joint forces and torques $\tau$ is equal to the time derivative of the partial derivative of the Lagrangian $L$ w.r.t. $\dot{\theta}$ minus the partial derivative of $L$ w.r.t. $\theta$. $$ \tau = \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial{L}}{\partial{\dot{\theta}}} - \frac{\partial{L}}{\partial{\theta}} $$ Vector equation in its components form: $$ \tau_i = \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial{L}}{\partial{\dot{\theta_i}}} - \frac{\partial{L}}{\partial{\theta_i}} $$ Power consumed or produced by joints $= \tau \cdot \dot{\theta}$

Newton-Euler formulation

This method relies on '$F=ma$' applied to each individual link of the robot. It uses some geometric tools, and it results in an efficient recursive algorithm for calculating the inverse dynamics.
