Variance

Variance is the average of the squared differences from the Mean. The way to calculate the variance:
  • Find the mean
  • For each number: subtract the mean and square the result (the squared difference)
    • Subtracting the mean makes the mean the zero point. It is a shift operation. We can now represent everything w.r.t the mean as simple deviations as shown here. Squaring the differences amplifies the deviation from the mean when we calculate the average in the next step. This is useful when we compare 2 different distributions - we then have just 1 number representing each distribution that contains all the information necessary to determine how spread out each distribution is from its mean.

  • Find the average of the squared differences

Standard deviation

It is a measure of how spread out numbers are. $$\displaylines{ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum^{N}_{i=1}(x_i-\mu)^2} \\ Variance = \sigma^{2} }$$

Z-score

It is the number of standard deviations from the mean. Steps in calculating:
  • For each number: subtract the mean, i.e. centre the distribution at the mean. This difference will give us each number's deviation from the mean.
  • Given a deviation, to find out how many standard deviations that is, divide it by a single standard deviation.
    • $$ zScore = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma} $$ It is similar to mapping 100seconds to the equivalent number of minutes. Since a minute is 60s, we divide 100 by 60 to get the answer. Here, each x-axis division is a standard deviation (denominator). The numerator is the deviation from the mean along the x-axis scale. Love the illustration here. I like the example here.

Definitions related to probability

Event or outcome Something that could happen
Sample space List of events
Discrete sample space Discrete set of events or possible outcomes
Probability Relative likelihood or relative frequency of an event occurring
Probability distribution (PD) List of events with their associated probabilities. It can be written as a function that maps from the outcomes to their corresponding probabilities
Normalised PD A PD in which all the probabilities sum to 1

Probability mass function When the outcomes are discrete, the PD function is called a probability mass function

Example

Let's think about whether a vehicle is working or not working: $$ P(working) = \frac{Frequency\, of\, working} {Total\, number\, of\, events} = \frac{9}{9+1} = 0.9 $$ Because this is a discrete sample space, we can write it in the form of a table.

Expected value

It is often referred to as the expectation, the mean, the average, or the first moment. The expected value is, on any given draw from a distribution, is the value we would expect to get? Not the most likely, but the average value that we'd expect to get if we were to continue drawing from this distribution over and over again. It gives the long-run average value of a repeated probabilistic experiment. $$ E(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}x_iP(X=x_i) $$ It's the integer value of the random variable, times the likelihood of each of those outcomes all summed together.

The expected value of a random variable, where the underlying events are truly non-numeric quantities such as working or not working, may not be useful. In the above example, if we compute the expected value of that variable X, it's going to be equal to 0.15. What that means is really unclear.

But imagine our sample space is actually a numeric quantity, such as the number of GPS satellites that a flying car can see or the birds that we can spot at any given time. Ref. The expected value tells us something about what we might expect to see at any given time.

Variance

When we are drawing from a distribution over and over again, the variance tells us how spread out the samples (the values being drawn) will be around the mean. It measures how spread out the numbers are from the mean (the expected value). People sometimes use the variance as a measure of how well we know the mean, something like a confidence. It's important to understand it isn't that, but it will tell us how much the samples vary. $$ \sigma^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{N}(x_i-\mu)^2P(X=x_i) $$ I had asked this question on stackexchange.
