Types of friction

Static friction

If an object is at rest w.r.t. a surface, horizontal forces up to a certain threshold force can be applied to the object and it will still remain static. Experiments show this threshold force is a constant (coefficient of static friction) times the normal force. $$\begin{align*} F_s \, > \, &\mu_s N \\ \frac{1}{3} \, \lt \, &\mu_s \lt 1 \end{align*}$$ This means that the force required to move the object horizontally is always less than the force with which gravity pulls the object down.

The mass of the bot is accounted for in the normal force. IRL, the threshold to start moving can never be smaller than $\frac{1}{3}$rd of the normal force even on a very slippery surface.

Kinetic friction

A certain force needs to be applied to keep an object moving at a constant velocity. This force is the same as the force of friction. From experiments, it is a constant (coefficient for kinetic friction) times the normal force (here, gravity). $$ F_k = \mu_k N $$ $F_k$ is not a threshold force.

Static vs kinetic friction

To make braking easier, it is more efficient to design a system where $\mu_s$ is larger than $\mu_k$, i.e. more force is required to get an object to start moving from rest compared to keeping it moving at a constant velocity. In other words, less force is required to stop an object when it is in the static regime (wheels stick more or have more instantaneous moments of stickiness?) or more force is required to stop an object when it is moving with some constant velocity.

Normally, $\mu_k \lt \lt \frac{1}{4}\mu_s$. To brake quicker, the maximum amount of friction (which is found in the static regime) needs to be applied because $\mu_s > \mu_k$.

However, the locking of wheels needs to be prevented. When a wheel stays locked, it slides on the ground (it moves with the momentum already built up), thereby staying in the regime of kinetic friction.

Wheel slip

[Good video on balance of forces on a wheel, Ref 2]
How does the distinction between static friction and kinetic friction apply to a rolling wheel? Say, a wheel has moved (rolled or dragged) along a road by a certain distance $x$. If the wheel perfectly rotates along the road, it would rotate by that same circumference (arc length). But, in braking there will always be a certain amount of slippage between the road and the wheel. Even though the wheel moves by $x$, it will have rotated by a distance less than $x$. $$\begin{align*} \scriptsize\text{wheel slip} &= \frac{\scriptsize\text{Missing rotation circumference length}}{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved}} \\ &= \frac{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved} - \scriptsize\text{Rotated circumference length}}{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved}} \\ &= 1- \frac{\scriptsize\text{Rotated circumference length}}{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved}} \end{align*}$$ When perfectly rolling, $\scriptsize\text{Missing rotation circumference length} == 0, \scriptsize\text{wheel slip} == 0$. When perfectly slipping, $\scriptsize\text{Missing rotation circumference length} == \scriptsize\text{Distance moved}, \scriptsize\text{wheel slip} == 1$
