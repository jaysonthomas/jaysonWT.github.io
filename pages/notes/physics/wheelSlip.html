Types of friction

Static friction

If an object is at rest w.r.t. a surface, we can apply horizontal forces up to a certain threshold force and the object will still remain static. Experiments show this threshold force is a constant times the normal force. The coefficient is called the coefficient of static friction. $$\begin{align*} F_s \, > \, &\mu_s N \\ \frac{1}{3} \, \lt \, &\mu_s \lt 1 \end{align*}$$ This means that the force required to move the object horizontally is always less than the force with which gravity pulls the object down.

The mass of the bot is accounted for in the normal force. In real life, the threshold to start moving can never be smaller than $\frac{1}{3}$ of the normal force even on a very slippery surface (?). Does $\mu_s$ depend on the materials of the interacting surfaces?

Kinetic friction

We need to apply a certain force to keep the object going at a constant velocity. This force is the same as the force of friction. From experiments, it is a constant (coefficient for kinetic friction) times the normal force (here, gravity). $$ F_k = \mu_k N $$ $F_k$ is not a threshold force.

For braking $\mu_s$ tends to be larger than $\mu_k$. Normally, $\mu_k \lt \lt \frac{1}{4}\mu_s$. We want to apply the maximum amount of friction which is found in the static regime, because $\mu_s > \mu_k$. What we need to prevent is the locking of wheels. When a wheel stays locked, it's sliding on the ground (it's moving with the momentum we already built up), and we're in the regime of kinetic friction.

Wheel slip

How does the distinction between static friction and kinetic friction apply to a rolling wheel? Say, a wheel has moved (rolled or dragged) along a road by a certain distance $x$. If the wheel perfectly rotates along the road, it would rotate by that same circumference (arc length). But, in braking there will always be a certain amount of slippage between the road and the wheel. Even though the wheel moves by $x$, it will have rotated by a distance less than $x$. $$\begin{align*} \scriptsize\text{wheel slip} &= \frac{\scriptsize\text{Missing rotation circumference length}}{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved}} \\ &= \frac{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved} - \scriptsize\text{Rotated circumference length}}{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved}} \\ &= 1- \frac{\scriptsize\text{Rotated circumference length}}{\scriptsize\text{Distance moved}} \end{align*}$$ When perfectly rolling, $\scriptsize\text{Missing rotation circumference length} == 0, \scriptsize\text{wheel slip} == 0$. When perfectly slipping, $\scriptsize\text{Missing rotation circumference length} == \scriptsize\text{Distance moved}, \scriptsize\text{wheel slip} == 1$
