Two-link arm: Kinematics overview

Forward Kinematics

$$ X^B = f^B_{kin}(q) $$ In this mathematical equation (actual implementation in code is different):
$q$ is the vector of positions.
$X$ is the pose in the world.
$f_{kin}$ is the FK of the body.
$B$ stands for body.
$G$ stands for gripper if it was used instead of $B$.

The mapping from joint positions to end-effector poses is called the Forward Kinematics problem. In the case of revolute joints, this mapping from joint angles to ee pose is non-linear. Perhaps, the simplest example of it is provided below (just the rotation part of the pose): $$ R(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix} c & -s \\ s & c \end{bmatrix} $$

Inverse Kinematics

Inverse kinematics is a nonlinear complicated function that helps to go from poses in the world to joint positions. $$ q = (f^B)^{-1}(X^B) $$ FK is easier since the mapping in IK is not always unique:
  • There can be multiple solutions ($q$) that get the same pose in the world.
  • There could also be no solutions.
IK is oftentimes not used directly since the IK engine could give slightly different answers:
  • For very similar poses
  • When there are multiple joint angle solutions for the same ee pose
Both cases would involve arm movements that jump around to different solutions in very short time spans, for e.g. every 0.0001 seconds which wouldn't be good. IK is normally used with additional requirements for continuity/smoothness/centering etc.
