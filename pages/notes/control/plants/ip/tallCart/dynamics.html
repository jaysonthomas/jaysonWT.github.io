Gravity acting on the pole

The component of the pole's weight in the direction of the pole is $m^{\scriptsize{G}} g\,cos\theta$.

Balance of forces about the centre of masses

Cart

$$\begin{align*} \newcommand{\WaG}{^{\scriptsize{W}\hspace{-0.8em}}a^{\scriptsize{G}}} \sum F_{\hat{i},m^{\scriptsize{C}}} &= m^{\scriptsize{C}} \ddot{x} \,\hat{i} \\ &= (-kx -b\dot{x} + F_2\, cos\theta + F_1\, sin\theta) \,\hat{i} \end{align*}$$

Pole

$$\begin{align*} \sum F_{\hat{d},m^{\scriptsize{G}}} &= m^{\scriptsize{G}} \cdot \WaG \,\hat{d} \\ &= -F_1 \,\hat{d} + m^{\scriptsize{G}}g\,cos\theta \,\hat{d} \\ \sum F_{\hat{q},m^{\scriptsize{G}}} &= m^{\scriptsize{G}} \cdot \WaG \,\hat{q} \\ &= -F_2 \,\hat{q} - m^{\scriptsize{G}}g\,sin\theta \,\hat{q} \end{align*}$$ $^{\scriptsize{W}\hspace{-0.4em}}a^{\scriptsize{G}}\,\hat{d}$ is the centre of gravity or mass of the pole w.r.t. the world frame in the $\hat{d}$ direction.

Balance of moments about the centre of masses

The advantage of computing the balance of moments about A is that $F_1$ and $F_2$ don't create any moments about the point. Hoping to never have to evaluate them is futile, since they pop up in the sum of forces about $\hat{d}$ and $\hat{q}$ anyway. The number of terms in the sum of torques equation below does reduce though in the former case. $$\begin{align*} \sum \tau^G_{\hat{k}} &= \frac{d}{dt} H^G \\ &= F_2\,l \,\hat{k} \\ &= \frac{d}{dt} \biggl(I^G \begin{bmatrix}0 \\ 0 \\ \omega_z\end{bmatrix}\biggr) \\ &= I^G_{zz} \, \ddot\theta \, \hat{k} \\ &= \frac{m_G\, l^2}{12}\,\ddot\theta \, \hat{k} \end{align*}$$ $\frac{d}{dt} H^G$ is the time rate of change of angular momentum about G.

$\sum \tau^G_{\hat{k}}$ is the sum of external torques about G. The only external torque is caused by $F_2$ about a moment arm of length $l$. Gravity causes no torque since it is acting downwards at G. The torque due to $F_2$ is positive when $F_2$ causes a rotation in the counter-clockwise $\hat{k}$ direction.
