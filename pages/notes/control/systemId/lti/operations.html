LTI: Physical parameters interaction

Allowable operations

If an LTI system is modeled with the below 6 operations, then assertions can be made about how the output of the system will change based on changes to the input, i.e. the system will behave in predictable ways.
  • Multiply or divide the input by a constant
    • $$\begin{align*} a \cdot x(t) \\ \frac{1}{a} \cdot x(t) \end{align*}$$
  • Integrate or differentiate the input
    • $$\begin{align*} \int{x(t)} \\ \frac{dx(t)}{dt} \end{align*}$$
  • Add or subtract multiple inputs
    • $$\begin{align*} x_1(t) + x_2(t) \\ x_1(t) - x_2(t) \end{align*}$$

Solution of an LTI ODE

In the real world, many physical parameters are related to each other through differential equations, and for LTI systems those become ODEs [Ref].

The solution of an ODE that is used to model an LTI system, can only consist of sinusoids and exponentials; since they are the only wave forms that don't change shape when subjected to any combination of the six legal operations.

Example: The derivative of a sinusoid is still a sinusoid. The integral of an exponential is still an exponential. Hence, the two wave forms would be the solution to equations that have the form: $$ ẍ + ẋ + x = 0 $$ The integral of a square wave, for example, is a sloping step pattern and not another square wave. Hence, defining an LTI system's impulse response in terms of sinusoids and exponentials, makes sense.

The ubiquity of these types of physical relationships is why the Laplace transform is one of the most important techniques in system analysis.
