Two-link Arm

Forward Kinematics

The end-effector position is given as: $$\begin{align*} x &= f_x(l_1, l_2, \theta_1, \theta_2) \\ &= l_1\,\cos(\theta_1) + l_2\,\cos(\theta_1+\theta_2) \\ y &= f_y(l_1, l_2, \theta_1, \theta_2) \\ &= l_1\,\sin(\theta_1) + l_2\,\sin(\theta_1+\theta_2) \end{align*}$$

The Jacobian

The Jacobian defines the relationship between operational space (end-effector) and joint space (joint angle) velocities. $$\begin{align*} \dot{x} &= \frac{df(x)}{dt} \\ &= \frac{\partial{f_x}}{\partial{\theta_1}} \frac{d\theta_1}{dt} + \frac{\partial{f_x}}{\partial{\theta_2}} \frac{d\theta_2}{dt} \\ &= -(l_1 s_1 + l_2 s_{12})\dot{\theta}_1 - l_2 s_{12} \dot{\theta}_2 \end{align*}$$ A similar equation can be written for $\dot{y}$. $\frac{d}{dt}cos = -sin$ $$\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{x} \\ \dot{y} \end{bmatrix} &= \begin{bmatrix} -y & -l_2 s_{12} \\ x & l_2 c_{12} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \dot{\theta}_1 \\ \dot{\theta}_2 \end{bmatrix} \\ &= J \begin{bmatrix} \dot{\theta}_1 & \dot{\theta}_2 \end{bmatrix} ^T \end{align*}$$

Analytical Jacobian

Given the forward kinematics equation $x = FK(q)$, the relationship between operational space and joint space velocities is computed by taking the time derivative of the $FK$ function. This can also be written as: $$ \dot{x} = J_a \dot{q} $$ where $J_a$ is the analytical Jacobian.

Geometric Jacobian

Geometric Jacobian maps the joint velocities to the end-effector's linear and angular velocities. It is computed through an iterative method which calculates the link velocities from the base to the end-effector. Each column of the geometric Jacobian specifies the contribution of the corresponding joint velocity to the end-effector's velocities.

Whereas analytical Jacobian gives the time derivatives of Euler angles, geometric Jacobian gives the angular velocities in the base frame.

Force-torque relationship

Assuming a virtual movement at the robot's end-effector ($\Delta{x}$) due to a force at the end-effector, due to conservation of energy/power: $$ F^T \Delta{x} = \tau^T \Delta{q} $$ where $\tau$ represents the joint torques. Since $\Delta{x} = J \Delta{q}$: $$ F^T J \Delta{q} = \tau^T \Delta{q} \; \implies \; F^T J = \tau^T \; \implies \; \tau = J^TF $$

Pseudo-inverse Jacobian

Given $\dot{x} = J\dot{q}$, to find $\dot{q}$ from $\dot{x}$, the pseudo-inverse of $J$ needs to be computed since $J$ is not always invertible, i.e. it may be rectangular, having an unequal number of rows and columns.

The pseudo-inverse is a rectangular matrix. Assuming that $x$ is $m$-dimensional and $q$ is $n$-dimensional, $J^\dagger$ is $n\times m$.

Different conditions

If $n=m$, the psuedo-inverse is a square matrix and is the inverse of $J$. It means that if there is any solution for the joint space it is a unique solution.

If $n < m$, the rank of $J$ is $n$ - the joint variable vector lives in a lower dimension space than the operational space vector, i.e. $J$ is not full-rank. In this case, there can be configurations in the operational space for which there is no solution in the joint space, i.e. the pesudo-inverse becomes singular or non-invertible (determinant is zero).

If $n > m$, the joint space dimension is higher than the operational space one; for a configuration in the operational space, there can be more than one solution in the joint space. In this case, a null-space term, which maps any joint space vector to a zero vector in the operational space is used to accommodate the extra solutions.
