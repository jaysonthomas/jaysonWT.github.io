Commonality: Measurement equation

Measurement model

A measurement model encapsulated in the Jacobian $H$:
  • Projects a state into its measurement space or
  • Maps a state to a measurement

In the simplest form, if a measurement equation was linear, it would look like: $$ y = Hx + v $$

$z_t$ or $y_t$ represents an observation or a measurement made at timestep $t$. $H$ is the Jacobian matrix; it contains the partial derivatives of the measurements with respect to the parameter/state(s) $x$ of interest.

Fundamental principle

More the measurements, the more accurate the estimate will be.

If the error $v$ is known (we only know the distribution w.r.t. the unknown $x$), $x$ can be found: $Hx = y - v$. Or if $x$ is known, $v$ can be solved. Since neither is known, this becomes an estimation problem. A maximum likelihood estimation approach is used which, intuitively looks at the measurements and asks what state/parameter value is the most likely to yield the particular set of measurements.

An estimation problem boils down to figuring out the measurement model $H$ which is often the tricky bit.
