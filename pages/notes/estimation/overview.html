Estimation approaches: Introduction

Assuming constant state

The following approaches assume a constant state while collecting data for estimation.
  • Batch Least squares
    • It requires that measurements come in all at once. The measurement model $H$ maps a state vector of length $m$ to a measurement vector of $k$ individual measurements. $$ \underbrace{y}_{k \times 1} = \underbrace{H}_{k \times m} \cdot \underbrace{x}_{m \times 1} $$

  • Recursive least squares
    • Having a batch of stored measurements is computationally intensive, since the amount of computational resources needed to solve the normal equations grows with the measurement vector size. With a recursive estimator, since measurements come in one at a time, $H$ will just be a vector, not a matrix. $H$ will map a state to a single measurement. $$ \underbrace{y}_{1 \times 1} = \underbrace{H}_{1 \times m} \cdot \underbrace{x}_{m \times 1} $$

  • Non-linear leasts squares
    • The measurement model is some arbitrary function of the underlying state.

Adapting to changes in a state

To estimate a state that is changing, the Kalman filter and its variants called extended and unscented kalman filters can be used. For example, to estimate the position of a vehicle, a kalman filter helps incorporate the vehicle's motion into the state estimation process.
